$\renewcommand{\AA}{\mathbb A} \newcommand{\RR}{\mathbb R} \newcommand{\ZZ}{\mathbb Z} \newcommand{\NN}{\mathbb N} \newcommand{\QQ}{\mathbb Q} \newcommand{\CC}{\mathbb C} \newcommand{\FF}{\mathbb F} \newcommand{\PP}{\mathbb P} \newcommand{\e}{\varepsilon} \newcommand{\ball}[2]{(#1-#2,\,#1+#2)} \newcommand{\floor}[1]{\left\lfloor{#1}\right\rfloor} \newcommand{\ceil}[1]{\left\lceil{#1}\right\rceil} \newcommand{\norm}[1]{\left\lVert{#1}\right\rVert} \newcommand{\diff}{\operatorname{diff }} \newcommand{\disc}{\operatorname{disc }} \newcommand{\ord}{\text{ord}} \newcommand{\lcm}{\text{lcm}} \newcommand{\del}{\partial} \newcommand{\emp}{\varnothing} \newcommand{\divides}{\,|\,} \newcommand{\op}[1]{\operatorname{#1}} \newcommand{\mf}[1]{\mathfrak{#1}} \newcommand{\mc}[1]{\mathcal{#1}} \newcommand{\sgn}{\operatorname{sgn}} \newcommand{\refl}{\op{refl}} \newcommand{\UU}{\mathcal{U}} $Today I learned the definition of perfect fields, from \href{https://kconrad.math.uconn.edu/blurbs/galoistheory/perfect.pdf}{Keith Conrad}: $K$ is perfect if and only if all irreducible polynomials $K[x]$ are separable. (Separable means that it doesn't have double roots when factored in the algebraic closure.) So $\QQ$ is perfect because for $\pi(x)\in\QQ[x],$ we can look at $\gcd(\pi(x),\pi'(x)),$ which will have degree less than $\pi(x)$ and divide into $\QQ[x]$ but divide $\pi(x).$ If $\pi(x)$ is linear, then we're already done, but if $\deg\pi \gt 1,$ then $\deg\pi'=\deg\pi-1 \gt 0,$ so\[\deg\gcd(\pi,\pi') \gt 0,\]which is our contradiction to the irreducibility of $\pi.$ The necessary hypothesis we used was that $\QQ[x]$ has characteristic $0$ so that $\deg\pi'=\deg\pi-1$ by direct computation.
